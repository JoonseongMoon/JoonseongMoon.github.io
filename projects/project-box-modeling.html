Simple Box Modeling – Comparative Study Miami vs Pittsburgh

Conducted a parametric building envelope study using eQUEST's DOE-2 engine for Miami's hot-humid climate, comparing 6 to 8 options each for roof assemblies, ground slab constructions, and window glazing configurations, achieving a 3% site EUI reduction over the baseline model.

Type Graduate Coursework
Year 2026
Role Energy Modeler
Tool eQUEST / DOE-2
Climate Miami (1A) & Pittsburgh (5A)
Overview

Project Objective

This study builds a simple box energy model in eQUEST to systematically evaluate how different building envelope choices affect annual energy performance. Using Miami's hot-humid climate (ASHRAE Climate Zone 1A) as the primary test case and Pittsburgh (Climate Zone 5A) as a comparative reference, the project isolates the impact of three key envelope components through parametric simulation.

The goal was to identify which envelope configurations yield the greatest reductions in site Energy Use Intensity (EUI) relative to a code-compliant baseline, and to understand how those optimal choices shift between a cooling-dominated and a heating-dominated climate.

Methodology

Parametric Envelope Study

A single-zone rectangular box model served as the controlled test geometry. The baseline was calibrated to ASHRAE 90.1 minimum envelope requirements. Each envelope component was then varied independently while holding all other inputs constant, following a one-variable-at-a-time (OVAT) parametric approach.

Component 1 — Roof Assembly
6–8 Parametric Options
Varied insulation R-values, assembly types (metal deck, built-up, cool roof coating), and solar absorptance to evaluate their impact on cooling and heating loads.
Component 2 — Ground Slab Construction
6–8 Parametric Options
Tested slab insulation R-values, edge insulation depths, and configurations ranging from uninsulated to perimeter-only to full-slab insulation.
Component 3 — Window Glazing
6–8 Parametric Options
Compared U-factors, Solar Heat Gain Coefficients (SHGC), and glazing types including single-pane, double-pane, triple-pane, low-e, and spectrally selective configurations.
Results

Performance Analysis

Across all three component categories, the parametric results revealed patterns specific to Miami's hot-humid climate where cooling loads dominate year-round.

Roof Assembly (6 Iterations)

The baseline roof used a Metal Frame, >24 in o.c. with 3 in. polyurethane R-18, yielding a site EUI of 45.33 kBtu/ft²/yr. Iterations progressively increased insulation R-value (R-24, R-30, R-36) and switched to polyisocyanurate (R-42). The best performer was Iteration 5 (Metal Frame, 24 in o.c. with 6 in. polyisocyanurate R-42) at 45.29 kBtu/ft²/yr. Interestingly, Iteration 6 (Wood Advanced Frame) performed worse than the metal frame options, showing that frame material matters alongside insulation R-value.

Ground Slab (6 Iterations)

Slab iterations varied concrete thickness (4, 6, 8 in.), board insulation, and lightweight concrete cap layers. The first three uninsulated iterations (4–8 in. concrete, no board insulation) showed minimal EUI differences (45.29–45.30). Adding 1 in. polyisocyanurate R-7 with 1.25 in. LW concrete cap dropped EUI to 45.04. The best slab configuration used 4 in. concrete with 1.5 in. polyisocyanurate R-10.5 and LW concrete cap, achieving 45.01 kBtu/ft²/yr.

Window Glazing (5 Iterations)

Window glazing had the largest impact of all three components. The baseline used Double Clear/Tint glazing (site EUI 45.01). Switching to Single Reflective glass (Iteration 1) immediately dropped EUI to 43.88, a much larger single-step improvement than any roof or slab change. The best performer was Iteration 5, a combination of Double Low-E with Single Reflective glazing, achieving 43.65 kBtu/ft²/yr, a reduction driven primarily by lower Solar Heat Gain Coefficient (SHGC).

Key Findings

Outcomes

Final comparison of Baseline (45.01 kBtu/ft²/yr) vs. Best combined envelope (43.65 kBtu/ft²/yr) with 3.01% site EUI reduction, 3.12% electricity reduction, and 3.11% total cost savings

Performance Summary

  • Achieved a 3.01% reduction in site EUI (45.01 → 43.65 kBtu/ft²/yr) and a 3.12% reduction in electricity consumption (686,740 → 665,320 kWh) compared to the baseline
  • Window glazing had the largest impact, where switching from Double Clear/Tint to Double Low-E + Single Reflective dropped EUI by 1.36 kBtu/ft²/yr, more than roof and slab changes combined
  • Roof assembly changes showed diminishing returns beyond R-30, with the best option (Metal Frame 24 in o.c., polyisocyanurate R-42) only marginally improving over R-36
  • Ground slab insulation had minimal sensitivity for uninsulated options (4–8 in. concrete), but adding board insulation with LW concrete cap provided a measurable improvement
  • Total annual cost savings of $3,212 ($103,287 → $100,075) from the combined best-case envelope

Miami vs Pittsburgh

The comparative framing highlights how optimal envelope strategies are climate-dependent. In Pittsburgh (CZ 5A), higher opaque insulation R-values and low-U glazing dominate the priority list, while SHGC matters far less due to lower solar intensity and a significant heating season. In Miami, the inverse is true. Minimizing solar gain through low SHGC and reflective glazing is far more valuable than maximizing insulation R-value alone.
